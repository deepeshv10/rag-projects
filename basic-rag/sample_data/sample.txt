Arjun of the High White Silence

The Himalayas did not merely surround Arjun’s village—they defined it. Snow-capped peaks rose like ancient sentinels, their shadows stretching across the valley at dawn, their silence heavier than any sound. The villagers believed the mountains were alive, listening, remembering. Arjun had grown up believing that too, because the mountains had shaped every moment of his life.
His home was a stone-and-wood house perched on a ridge above the Alaknanda River, where prayer flags fluttered endlessly, whispering wishes into the wind. Arjun was twenty-six, lean from years of climbing steep paths and chopping wood at high altitude. His dark eyes held the stillness of glacial lakes, and his hands were calloused not just from labor, but from loss.
Arjun’s father had been a shepherd, guiding flocks across high pastures where grass pierced through snow like stubborn hope. One winter, when Arjun was twelve, his father never returned from the mountains. An avalanche—swift, merciless—had erased him from the world. Since then, Arjun had carried a quiet understanding: the mountains gave life, but they took it just as easily.
Yet, he stayed.
Every morning before sunrise, Arjun climbed to a rocky outcrop overlooking the valley. He brewed butter tea over a small fire and watched the first light touch the peaks of Nanda Devi and Trishul. In those moments, the Himalayas glowed pink and gold, as if forgiving the world for its flaws. Arjun felt closest to his father then.
The village relied on Arjun more than he admitted. He guided pilgrims along treacherous trails, rescued stranded trekkers, and carried medicine through blizzards when roads disappeared. Outsiders called him brave. Arjun thought of himself as necessary.
One winter, heavier than any before, the snow arrived early. The sky hung low, swollen with clouds, and the air carried a strange tension. Elders whispered that the mountains were restless.
One evening, as the wind howled through deodar trees, a stranger arrived.
She was barely conscious when Arjun found her near the frozen riverbank, half-buried in snow. Her clothes were not meant for the altitude, her fingers blue with cold. Without hesitation, Arjun lifted her onto his back and carried her home, fighting the wind with every step.
For three days, the storm trapped them inside. Arjun fed her warm broth, wrapped her in wool blankets, and listened as she slowly returned to life. Her name was Meera, a climate researcher from Delhi, sent to study glacial retreat. She spoke softly, with wonder and fear in equal measure.
“The glaciers are melting faster than anyone predicted,” she said one night, staring into the fire. “The mountains are changing.”
Arjun said nothing. He already knew.
When the storm passed, Meera insisted on continuing her work. Against his better judgment, Arjun agreed to guide her to the glacier above the village. The path was steep and dangerous, but Meera walked with determination, stopping often to take notes and photographs.
At the glacier’s edge, silence swallowed them. The ice wall loomed above, cracked and groaning like something wounded.
“It’s dying,” Meera whispered.
The sound that followed was not a whisper.
A thunderous crack split the air. The glacier shifted, sending shards of ice cascading down the slope. Arjun grabbed Meera’s arm and ran. The earth trembled beneath their feet as snow and ice chased them downhill.
They barely escaped.
Later, as they sat shaking in the safety of a cave, Arjun felt something inside him break open—not fear, but resolve.
“If the glacier falls,” he said, “the river will flood the village.”
Meera nodded. “You need to leave. All of you.”
The village elders refused at first. The Himalayas had been their home for generations. Leaving felt like betrayal. But the signs grew impossible to ignore. Cracks in the ice. Rising waters. Unstable slopes.
Arjun spoke to them, his voice steady.
“The mountains are not angry,” he said. “They are warning us.”
In the end, they listened.
The evacuation was slow and painful. Families packed what they could carry. Prayer flags were left behind, fluttering alone in the wind. As the last villagers descended the trail, Arjun stayed back, standing at the edge of the village.
That night, the glacier collapsed.
The roar echoed across the valley like the cry of an ancient beast. Snow, ice, and water thundered down, swallowing fields, homes, memories. When the sun rose, the village was gone.
Months later, Arjun stood in a resettlement town far below the mountains. Life there felt crowded, noisy, unmoored. But the villagers were alive.
Meera visited before returning to the city. She thanked Arjun, not just for saving her life, but for saving the village.
“What will you do now?” she asked.
Arjun looked north, where the Himalayas rose faintly in the distance.
“I will return,” he said. “The mountains still need witnesses.”
Years later, trekkers spoke of a solitary guide who lived high above the tree line, teaching respect for the land, rescuing the careless, and listening to the silence. They said he moved like the wind, spoke little, and carried the wisdom of stone.
Arjun had not left the Himalayas after all.
He had become part of them.
